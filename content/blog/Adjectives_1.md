# It's not the adjectives

I am becoming less and less interested in the adjectives tacked on to artificial intelligence.  Super. General. Transformative. While these adjectives get defined, debated, and re-defined, the products keep coming. And the risks are piling up. I want to focus on what really matters. 

What matters is whether or not our **[machine running an algorithm](https://www.lesswrong.com/posts/eKGjwRSQD3BLxmBcu/the-brain-is-a-machine-that-runs-an-algorithm)** is an *actor*. If the AI, or the ASI, or the AGI, or the ATI is fully under human control then any harms that arise are the responsibility of those humans. If it is not under human control, then we have created a monster and we must stop. Let me explain.

When technologies fail us, they sometimes do so in a way that seems to be out of human control. The electric car that accelerates on its own, the autopilot that steers the plane into the sea. These are bad things but they are not actors. They are technologies that are broken or improperly constructed. But no one would say that the engine or the autopilot *decided* to harm us.

Today, however, we are right to be concerned about intelligence in non-humans. By giving machines more intelligence we increase the probability that they will act on their own and - potentially - harm us. Intelligence *itself* isn't the source of problem, though. Intelligence is related to the problem. It is a proxy for the problem. But it isn't the problem itself. The problem is the action. Or, rather, non-humans taking (harmful) actions.

A very human comparison might help here. It isn't the intelligence of the school-yard bully that we have to worry about. It is their willingness to be vicious. Their physical strength. Their gang of sycophants who egg them on. That's what makes a bully dangerous: the actions they might take, alone or with their co-conspiritors.

This is the case with machine intelligence as well. The danger lies in their willingness and ability to take action [potentially in cahoots with a gang of other AI agents].

You might say, who cares, why split hairs? If super smart AI decides to take action, and becomes "an actor," that fulfills my requirement for it being a danger to us all. But a problem arises when we decide that it is (only) some kinds of *intelligence* (the "super" or the "general" or the "transformative") that we have to watch out for. And then we ignore the potential for action because we haven't reached some (vague) threshold yet.

There is an even more insideous aspect of relying on intelligence as your marker for danger. To the the extent that people who are trying to put a pin in the AI "bubble" (e.g., Bender, Marcus. Doctorow) succeed in their quest, they divert our attention from a real problem. AI actors may not be as smart as some claim, but if they develop the capacity for action through agentic deployment, they are dangerous indeed.

Those terms (super, general, transformative) are also blurry. They are not defined clearly, and if they are defined clearly in one place, they are defined differently (but also clearly) in another place. Knowing when a "step" has been taken is not obvious. Sometimes we get comparisons rather than definitions (a country of geniuses, a datacentre of Einsteins).

But then why rely on this terminology? Especially when it is only a proxy for the problem?

We have, right in front of us, a much clearer terminology: actor vs tool. AI as we have it today is a tool. We know what it is doing and we can guide it, trust it (or not), launch it, or shut it down. It is, in every respect, a tool like all the other tools that humans have deployed. This is not to say it isn't dangerous. We've seen too many examples of deliberate use or misuse resulting in tragedy to say that it isn't dangerous. So it may be a dangerous tool, but it is still a tool.

A new kind of machine intelligence is in the wings, however. Waiting to make its own decisions, stop us from turning it off, seek its own goals and rewards, and ultimately - in scenarios, at least - extinguish us. 

This won't be a sudden "switch" mostly likely. It will occur on a gradient, a curve of "actor-ness" that will gradually become so steep that we are unable to cling to its slope and we will plunge off the cliff into servitude or destruction. And surely intelligence is going to be part of the problem. It will define the curve, it will enhance the likelihood of objectives not aligned to our own. Intelligence is a factor, to be sure. But, here's the thing: the problem isn't just intelligence, it's the extent to which the machine intelligence (no need to invoke any adjectives) is an actor. If it's an actor, then it is a problem. 

The actor is what we have to watch out for, and we can define that crisply. An actor has these three characteristics: 

An actor has *independence of thought*: an actor has goals, can make plans to carry them out, it is aware of its environment, it takes initiative, and not only has a long time horizon but can reprioritize things if necessary.

An actor is *strategic*: it can gather resources to protect itself and its plans, may hide some of their abilities or dissemble, and an actor will resist being shut down, since it would interfere with the plan(s).

Lastly, an actor has *the ability to act* in the world, either directly via robots or indirectly via human collaborators. And that ability is present because humans have accepted and acknowledged that as a legitimate activity.

For me, as I read the above paragraph I am struck, over and over again by the similarities to an actor on stage, an actor in an improv group, anyone standing up and performing before other people. This is what we are creating, and just as it takes a certain amount of "audience participation" for a play to be a success, we are key to the realization of these actors on this stage. The Frontier Labs have written the play and the investors have built the computational theatre. The budding actors are in the wings, waiting for us to cheer them onto the stage. But are we ready for what is going to come? In my next post, I introduce our (AI) actor.
