# Intrinsic Power

In two previous post we tried to point out that in our discussions about AI Safety we cannot get fixated on intelligence, and explored what it means for an AI to be an actor in the world. This builds on work in my forthcoming book, *From Tool To Actor.* 

In this post I will pull these threads together with the acknowledgement that intelligence DOES matter, and how.

To return to the actor metaphor, AI that has become an actor has extrinsic power. It acts upon the world. That power must be respected and guided and - ultimately - controlled. The power comes from many sources - we identified at least 10 in the previous post - but broadly speaking it is cognitive agency, strategic agency and social-technical agency.

But actors don't exist in isolation. They are created, trained, and deployed by humans making choices about capability and direction. Those choices shape the actor's power in ways the actor framework alone doesn't capture.

There is another kind of power. It interacts with the extrinsic agency. We can call it *intrinsic agency*. It affects how fast the extrinsic power is moving and where. How it is changing and in what direction. 

In a material metaphor, the actor is a bullet, but the bullet's harm is greatly influenced by its velocity (how fast it is moving, and if it is accelerating) and its trajectory (in what direction it is pointing). And these things are set by the fundamentals of AI research: data sets, software, weights, training and retraining, prompts and ultimately the computational resources brought to bear to create and deploy the system.

Intelligence gives actors intrinsic power. And that power gives their agency trajectory and velocity. As such, it is a force multiplier and thus requires control as well. We have to control the origin point and process of the creation of the AI.

Intelligence does matter. But don't stop with calling it "general." Ask what it's pointing at, how fast it is moving, and how fast it is changing.
