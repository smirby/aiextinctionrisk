Keeping up. Part two.

I realized, after I "finished" the piece on keeping up [insert link here], that I had actually strayed so far into the past that I forgot what prompted me to start writing in the first place. So, I am starting again. Or, rather, I am writing a second part.

In the first episode (really? Episodes?) I described my early mode of keeping up: magazine subscriptions. Let's' jump ahead a decade, and we will see that I am still following that path but I've left cars (for now) and scuba gear (forever) and am instead focused on "tech." 

I wasn't always a techie. I remember, in my undergraduate program, having to use the SPSS program on a paper terminal (no steekin CRTs for us!) and being completely uninterested in the technology behind it. Though I did retain a functional ability with statistics, sufficient for my MA and PhD course work, anyway, and a few part-time jobs for professors while I was a grad student.

Anyway, by the time I finished my MA (1985), computers were finding their ways out of the mainframe era and onto people's desktops. There were TRS-80 and Commodore 64 home computers, and the new IBM PC and of course the Apple II and Apple Mac. My interest in these was supported by a "keeping up" practice of reading Byte magazine. And, it was while reading that magazine that I came to hear about the world of online networks. While I didn't realize it at the time, this was going to drive my "keeping up" habits for decades to come.

I learned about modems and connecting a computer to a host. Our university had the ability and - since we lived quite a ways from campus - I thought it would be cool to connect up to the mainframe (Michigan Terminal System, or MTS, running on IBM hardware, I believe) and write my thesis that way, rather than driving to the school. Once I had a modem, I started using it to connect to smaller, local services, called Bulletin Board Systems, or BBS's.

I have no recollection of what triggered it, but I ended up paying for a second telephone line so that I could have a connection to the Fidonet, and eventually ran my own BBS (Opus software, called "[Tusk_Net](https://nodehist.fidonet.org.ua/?address=1%3A153%2F136)", zone 1, net 153, node 136), if I recall correctly. [[List of Vancouver BBSs](http://bbslist.textfiles.com/604/oldschool.html)]. This list, from 1997, shows EPIX (Emergency Preparedness Information EXchange) at 1:153/162, that I helped run for a professor in our department, Pete Anderson. https://stuff.mit.edu/afs/athena/reference/net-directory/host-tables/FIDONet-Hosts.txt 

Somewhere around this time I came to realize that I could do my keeping up via computer. Initially this was mostly related to the software that we used on the computer itself or the sofware to connect to the modem, dial the numbers, and (eventually) host a system that people could dial in to.

Since most people wanted to dial into a system that had something to offer, and I wasn't interested in hosting "free" software or images, I settled on MIDI files. MIDI is a protocol for managing music and musical instruments via computer. I actually knew hardly anything about this topic - and still don't, really - but the technician in our department gave me a bunch of floppy disks with MIDI software on them, and that was the start of my bulletin board system. Along with files to download, there was the ability to leave messages, in a group discussion or personal messages. Not unlike posting on Facebook or sending an email, just incredibly limited in scope. 

The "Fidonet" system did allow you to have a broader discussion, and you could - in theory - send messages between systems via an overnight store and forward system, though it was lightly used as I recall. My computer would call, late at night in a window of time allocated for it, and exchange messages with another computer "up the chain" from me, and so one and so forth throughout the system.

Anyway, I am getting very distracted from the purpose of this post, which is keeping up. I kept up with the BBS world, by being a BBS and tinkering with the system to implement updates and so on. I didn't do any real computer programming, though I did some "scripting" (batch files and so on), which also required keeping up and tinkering. I guess that's a secondary aspect to keeping up; doing the keeping up that helps you make things. Perhaps not "mastery" where you can make things from scratch, but enough skill to assemble or disassemble and then reassemble.

Parallel to the BBS world, I was gradually learning about the internet. When it was emerging - a purely academic project at the time - but not yet available in Vancouver, I read about it in Byte Magazine and wished that I could take part. The university had a store and forward network system ("BITnet") that could be used to send messages from computer to computer within that network. There was also a unix computer network system (usenet) that existed on campus but was pretty much only for those in computer science.

Somehow, I got myself into the circle of people who were going to set up an internet-connected computer at my university, and link it to the next closest computer at a neighbouring university and from there it would be connected (via modem, if you can imagine) to the University of Washington, in Seattle. We all got accounts on that computer (that's why, to this day, I have "smith@sfu.ca" as an email address; I was there at the very beginning). The computer we used as a Vax or Sun computer and it was in the Laboratory for Computer Communication Research (LCCR) at SFU. We had to use a special dial-in number for that, and I had to learn unix commands and the terminal #shell system.

Once I was connected to the internet, my keeping up interests was completely saturated. I wanted to keep up with everything. It seems weird to say it now, but the internet was still a pretty small place in those days and much of what was going on was between individuals. Group things, even mailing lists, were pretty small. A couple of notable things: I joined a group called "com-priv" which was dedicated to discussions about what might happen if we allowed "commercialization" and "private enterprise" on the internet. This was probably in 1990. 

That this was a debate will probably surprise people today, but in those days it was a hard line, no commercial speech kind of place. You could get in trouble by talking about having a car for sale. I kid you not. I just did a pretty thorough search on the internet to see if I can find any vestige of those old mailing lists but there is nothing.

The group-oriented messaging on the Internet, back in those days, was "net news," a spin-off or superset of "usenet news," which had been circulating for a while in the usenet community of (mainly) computer scientists. There was a master list of topics and you could subscribe to a topic so that the next time you opened up the netnews application on your terminal ("nn" was the program you ran), it would list the new items. There were a lot of discussion focums/topics but it was a list that you could actually skim through, mark the ones you were interested in, and - assuming you didn't subscribe to too many or they weren't too verbose you could actually "keep up" with what the WHOLE INTERNET was saying on a topic that day.

To even say this sounds ludicrous now. Perhaps crazier than that we were actually debating if/when there would be commercial speed on the internet. But we did and it was like that. And I did subscribe to MANY groups and topics and dutifully tried to keep up, as best I could, with what was going on. As you can imagine, that practice became more and more onerous as time went on.
