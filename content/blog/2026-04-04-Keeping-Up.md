# Keeping up

I have always enjoyed "keeping up." In my early and mid-teens we lived in an information desert. At least, compared to today that is. Back then (early 1970s), I had no idea what I was missing so I just did what it took to fill the - in retrospect - gap in information in my life.

In those days, news was disseminated in newspapers and magazines. And radio and television, I guess, but those didn't really allow you to dig deep into a subject, which was what I really wanted. Newspapers were too general, as well. What I wanted was a "deep dive." I found that in magazines. 

To promote new subscriptions, there was a think called "[Publishers Clearinghouse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Publishers_Clearing_House)." It delivered colourful packets of information on magazines that you could subscribe to, at deep discounts. Presumably to lure you in and get you to continue paying the regulat subscription rate. There were sheets of tiny, postage-stamp sized magazine covers and you would tear out the one you wanted and lick the stamp and the afix it to the mail-in card. Your subscription would begin.

Similar systems were in place for books and records, and they often defaulted to a much higher priced regular monthly payment and a system of "if you don't return this card saying you don't want it, we'll assume you want it, and charge you for it." Many people fell with that, for a while anyway, and ended up with collections of books or LPs that they didn't want. The magazine thing was slightly better in that you got a full year at the discounted rate (99 cents?) before you started paying full price.

I should note here that we lived in the country and purchasing magazines - while possible on a trip to town - was not a very satisfying experience. There wasn't much to choose from and they were (relatively, to me anyway) expensive. So the Publishers Clearinghouse was my access window to focused, deep dive information.

Speaking of deep dives, one of my first subscriptions was to Skin Diver magazine. I read every issue, until my subscription ran out. I knew everything there was to know about wet suits, regulators, flippers, and masks. I definitely had a penchant for the gear. To this day I have never actually put on a tank and regulator and done any diving at all. I just wanted to learn new things, especially with a technical side.

After Skin Diver magazine I moved on to car magazines: Road & Track, Car & Driver. Never MotorTrend, though. I don't know why. I did learn to drive, of course, but I never had a sports car, never went in a race or a rally, didn't even modify the cars I drove (which belonged to my parents, so I would not have been allowed to change them anyway). 

Sometimes I carried on for some years, paying the actual subscription price and avidly awaiting my monthly dose of car or diving or whatever kind of information I was absorbing like a sponge in those days. Decades later I met a guy who grew up in New York City. He was the same age as me but as we compared childhoods I realized that instead of an information desert, he had grown up in an information rainforest. Replete with overgrowing tangles of rich and exotic information, not to mention art and architecture, operas and symphonies, libraries and museums everywhere. I guess that children today have something like that, a profusion of choices. I hope they find what they are looking for.
